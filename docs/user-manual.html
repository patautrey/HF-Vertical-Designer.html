HF Vertical Designer – User Manual

This User Manual provides a complete reference for the HF Vertical Designer. It explains every input, every calculation, and the RF theory behind the tool. Use this manual when you want to understand how the calculator models impedance, SWR, ERP, transformer behavior, and multi-band capability.

1. Overview

The HF Vertical Designer is a modeling engine for portable and fixed HF vertical antennas. It evaluates:

  • Antenna geometry (straight, foldover, helical)
  • Transformer / UNUN selection
  • Feedline type and length
  • Counterpoise and radial systems
  • Environmental effects (soil, saltwater, height)
  • SWR with and without a tuner
  • ERP per band
  • Radiation patterns (azimuth, elevation, donut)

2. Antenna Geometry

2.1 Element Length

The most important input. Determines electrical behavior across all HF bands. Examples:

  • 17.5 ft – ultra-portable, 20–6m
  • 20.5 ft – 5/8-wave on 10m
  • 25 ft – classic Rybakov, 40–6m
  • 29 ft – random-wire 9:1, 40–10m
  • 33 ft – 20m EFHW
  • 41 ft – 80–10m long vertical

2.2 Mast Height

Determines how much of the wire is vertical. If the wire exceeds mast height, use foldover or helical mode.

2.3 Foldover Mode

Models DX Commander–style geometry where the top of the wire bends downward. Vertical portion = mast height. Folded portion contributes partial electrical length.

2.4 Helical Mode

Models wrapping the top portion of the wire around the mast. This preserves electrical length while reducing physical height. Used for 33 ft EFHW on short poles or 41 ft verticals on 10 m poles.

2.5 Counterpoise

Required for EFHW and random-wire systems. Typical length: 5% of wavelength (e.g., 1 m for 20m EFHW).

3. Transformer / UNUN Selection

3.1 1:1 Balun

Used for vertical dipoles and OCF vertical dipoles.

3.2 4:1 UNUN

Used for Rybakov family: 17.5 ft, 20.5 ft, 25 ft.

3.3 9:1 UNUN

Used for 29 ft, 41 ft, and helical 33 ft random wires.

3.4 49:1 UNUN

Used for EFHW verticals (20m, 40m).

3.5 64:1 UNUN

Optimal for 15m EFHW (lower SWR, less core stress).

Tip: Wrong transformer ratio = high SWR + wasted power + hot cores.

4. Feedline Modeling

4.1 Coax Types

  • LMR‑400 – lowest loss
  • RG‑213 – field standard
  • RG‑8X – portable-friendly
  • RG‑58 – ultralight, highest loss

4.2 Ladder Line

Used for doublets and OCF systems. High VF, low loss, but requires a tuner.

4.3 Electrical Length

The tool computes electrical length in degrees based on VF and frequency.

4.4 Coax Loss

Loss is interpolated between 30 MHz and 50 MHz reference points.

5. Environment & Ground System

5.1 Soil Type

  • Saltwater – +3 dB low-angle gain
  • Good ground – +1 dB
  • Average – baseline
  • Inland poor soil – reduced efficiency

5.2 Radials

4–7 radials at ~16 ft dramatically improve efficiency for Rybakov/random-wire systems.

5.3 Time of Day

  • Day – higher-angle bias
  • Night – slightly lower angles
  • Grayline – enhanced low-angle propagation

6. Band Capability Detection

The tool evaluates each HF band (80m–6m) and classifies it as:

  • Usable – SWR ≤ 3 without tuner
  • Tuner – SWR ≤ 10 with tuner
  • Not matchable – SWR too high

6.1 EFHW Harmonics

EFHW verticals are naturally resonant on harmonics:

  • 20m EFHW → 20m, 10m, 40m (with tuner)
  • 15m EFHW → 15m, 10m, 6m

6.2 Rybakov Family

Non-resonant lengths avoid impedance extremes and tune easily across many bands.

7. SWR Modeling

SWR is computed from:

  • Estimated feedpoint impedance
  • Transformer ratio
  • Coax transformation

7.1 With Tuner

The tool assumes a modern ATU can reduce SWR to ~1.5:1 on any band within matching range.

8. ERP Calculation

ERP is computed from:

  • TX power
  • Antenna gain (height + type)
  • Ground conductivity
  • Coax loss
  • SWR mismatch loss
Saltwater Example:
A 25 ft Rybakov at 100 W near saltwater can produce ERP equivalent to 200–300 W inland.

9. Radiation Patterns

The tool displays:

  • Azimuth (top-down)
  • Elevation (side-view)
  • Donut projection (3D pattern)

These are approximations based on antenna type and height.
